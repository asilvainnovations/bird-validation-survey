### 1. Shifting the Burden — Symptomatic Solutions vs. Structural Reform

**Diagram:** https://lydsisparsmvextskevw.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/validation-survey-images/Shifting%20the%20Burden%20Archetype.png

**Description:**

This archetype illustrates how short-term interventions in BARMM often *replace* deeper structural reforms rather than support them. Persistent challenges — such as low investment inflows, limited revenue generation, and weak institutional capacity — represent the core problem symptoms. Government and development partners typically respond with **symptomatic solutions**: ad hoc tax incentives, emergency relief programs, short-term security operations, and one-off project funding. These quick fixes temporarily reduce visible problems, which eases the pressure to pursue long-term reforms. But because root causes remain unaddressed, the same symptoms reappear — prompting another round of short-term fixes. Over time, this creates a **vicious reinforcing cycle**: dependency on external support deepens, local institutional capacity weakens, and the system becomes trapped in recurring crises. For example, tax incentives may attract investors briefly, but without fixing slow certification systems, poor infrastructure, and skills gaps, investment momentum fades once incentives expire. The result is a system that repeatedly treats symptoms rather than curing the underlying condition — trading temporary relief for long-term resilience.

### 2. Fixes That Fail — Short-Term Relief Incentives vs. Systemic Capacity Building

**Diagram:** https://lydsisparsmvextskevw.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/validation-survey-images/Fixes%20that%20Fail%20Archetype.png

**Description:**

This archetype captures the unintended consequences of relying on quick fixes to address deep systemic problems in BARMM. The region faces low investment approvals, weak institutional capacity across ministries (MTIT, MAFAR, MPW, MENRE), and fragmented program delivery. The typical response is a **"quick fix"**: ad hoc tax incentives, short-term security operations, fragmented agricultural subsidies, and piecemeal infrastructure projects. These measures create the *illusion of progress* — investments may rise briefly, confidence improves, and symptoms appear to ease. But each cycle of short-term fixes **erodes institutional capacity further**: procurement delays persist (12–18 month lead times), halal certification remains slow (45–60 days vs. Malaysia's 15-day benchmark), and inter-agency coordination does not improve. Investors often exit once incentives expire. Fragmented subsidies without post-harvest or market-linkage infrastructure fail to create sustainable value. The system becomes trapped in a **recurring cycle of crisis management** rather than building the ecosystem needed for lasting development. Each "fix" makes the next crisis more likely, not less.

### 3. Success to the Successful — Winners Keep Winning, Regardless of Merit

**Diagram:** https://lydsisparsmvextskevw.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/validation-survey-images/Success%20to%20the%20Successful%20Aarchetype.png

**Description:**

This archetype reveals how **initial advantages create a self-reinforcing cycle of uneven development** across BARMM's provinces. Mainland provinces — particularly **Maguindanao del Norte** and **Lanao del Sur** — attract the bulk of resources, investments, and government services due to their larger economies, stronger infrastructure networks, and administrative centrality (Cotabato City, Polloc Freeport). This success justifies further investment, creating a virtuous cycle of growth (4.1% GRDP growth, ₱81.91B GDP in Maguindanao). In contrast, **island provinces** — Tawi-Tawi, Sulu, and Basilan — experience chronic underinvestment and logistical isolation despite holding significant comparative advantages: Tawi-Tawi produces ~40% of national seaweed output, Basilan has 48,366 hectares of rubber plantations, and both provinces offer direct BIMP-EAGA trade access. These island provinces suffer from limited cold-chain facilities, digital connectivity below 30%, minimal Islamic banking presence, and high logistical costs compounded by perceived security risks. Growth rates remain marginal (Tawi-Tawi 1.1%, Sulu 1.13%, Basilan 1.6%), and poverty incidence is highest where investment is lowest. The result is a **reinforcing loop where success breeds more success** — regardless of merit or need — widening regional inequality and undermining BARMM's vision of inclusive development.

### 4. Tragedy of the Commons — Resource Over-Exploitation in the Foundations Cluster

**Diagram:** https://lydsisparsmvextskevw.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/validation-survey-images/Tragedy%20of%20the%20Commons%20Archetype.png

**Description:**

This archetype captures the **over-exploitation of shared natural resources** across BARMM's Foundations cluster (agriculture, fisheries, forestry, energy, and environment). When governance remains fragmented and agencies operate in silos, individual actors — farmers, fishers, loggers, energy users — rationally maximize their short-term gains from shared resources such as watersheds, fishing grounds, forest reserves, and power grids. Each actor's individual gain is positive, so intensified resource use continues. However, the **collective impact** depletes the very ecological and infrastructural base that all downstream value creation depends upon. Two reinforcing loops drive this: (R1) increased extraction boosts individual economic gains, attracting more actors and intensifying resource use; and (R2) growing communities and industries place even greater pressure on finite resources. There is a **dangerous delay** between over-exploitation and visible depletion — by the time impacts are felt (e.g., forest degradation threatening hydropower, fishery collapse, El Niño-induced drought causing ₱346M in crop losses), the system may have already crossed irreversible thresholds. Weak balancing mechanisms mean individual actors continue extracting even as collective losses mount. Without governance intervention — such as the Bangsamoro Forestry Code, carbon markets, and community co-management — the Foundations cluster risks **systemic collapse**.

### 5. Cycles of Exclusion and Retaliation — Competitive Spirals and Escalation

**Diagram:** https://lydsisparsmvextskevw.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/validation-survey-images/Cycles%20of%20Exclusion%20and%20Retaliation.png

**Introduction Diagram:** https://lydsisparsmvextskevw.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/validation-survey-images/Instability%20Traps%20Introduction.png

**Description:**

This archetype — also called **"Escalation"** — manifests in both socio-political and economic domains in BARMM. When one group (clan, province, or agency) perceives a threat or marginalization, it mobilizes to protect its interests — through political consolidation, competing for the same investments, rido (clan feuds), or fragmented agency planning. Other groups perceive this mobilization as a threat to their own interests, triggering **counter-mobilization**. The result is a **reinforcing cycle of competitive spirals**: Group A acts → Group B retaliates → Group A escalates further → Group B responds in kind. This dynamic plays out across multiple arenas: clan rivalries over land and resources, inter-provincial competition for BARMM block grants, agencies competing for the same donor pools, and market competition from established halal hubs (Malaysia, Indonesia). The cycle diverts resources from productive development to contestation, degrades the overall investment climate, weakens BARMM's collective competitiveness in BIMP-EAGA, and creates a perception of instability that repels investors. Both sides end up worse off — but neither can unilaterally de-escalate without appearing weak.

### 6. The "Big Man" Archetype — Concentration of Power and Perpetual Instability

**Diagram:** https://lydsisparsmvextskevw.supabase.co/storage/v1/object/public/validation-survey-images/The%20Big%20Man%20Archetype.png

**Description:**

This archetype reveals how the **concentration of power around dominant political and clan leaders** — known as "Big Men" in BARMM — creates a self-reinforcing system of perpetual instability. At its center lies the Big Man's domination, from which three interlocking reinforcing loops emanate:

**R1 — The Patronage–Governance Trade-off:** The Big Man's concentration of power fosters a patron–client psychology among followers — the belief that clan survival depends on protecting the Big Man at all costs. Resources are diverted through inter-clan marriages, shadow-economy trading, and misuse of government funds. Favoritism and patronage politics become institutionalized, producing biased resource distribution. The priority of staying in power gradually overrides the demands of good governance, eroding public institutions while entrenching the Big Man's control.

**R2 — The Cycle of Exclusion, Resentment, and Conflict:** The Big Man's dominance inevitably antagonizes rival clans, intensifying competition and triggering struggles for power. This widens patterns of exclusion and resentment against both competing clans and vulnerable, non-aligned populations. Over time, exclusion breeds hopelessness expressed through **rido** (clan-based violent feuds) among rivals and **radicalism** among those outside the patronage network. The resulting violence destabilizes the political landscape, creating conditions that justify — or even necessitate — the Big Man's continued domination "in the name of clan survival."

**R3 — Resource Depletion and Development Failure:** Patronage-based hiring places unqualified individuals in government posts. Bureaucracies swell beyond efficiency. Public budgets are consumed by administrative bloat rather than productive investment. The level of resources available for genuine local development diminishes, heightening competition among political clans and fueling fiercer contestation — creating a reinforcing loop that traps the region in a **low-development equilibrium**.

Together, these three loops form a **vicious cycle** where political dominance perpetuates conflict, conflict justifies continued dominance, and both together drain the resources needed for development.
